Market Sizing TAM / SAM / SOM

When to use: Early-stage strategy projects, go-to-market decks, investor-facing slides.

Inputs to gather: Product description, target geography, pricing assumption, ICP definition.

Validation: Every number must trace to a row in the assumptions table with a graded source.

Prompt 
You are a consulting analyst. Estimate the TAM, SAM, and SOM for [PRODUCT]
in [GEOGRAPHY], serving [ICP], priced at [PRICE].

Use both methods:
1. Top-down: cite industry reports, name them explicitly.
2. Bottom-up: # of target customers × adoption rate × ACV.

Output format:
- Numbered assumption table: assumption | value | source | grade (A/B/C)
- TAM / SAM / SOM with the math shown
- Flag the 3 assumptions most likely to break the estimate and why.

If you cannot source a number, label it ESTIMATED and explain the logic.
Do not invent reports or statistics.

Customer Segmentation Growth

When to use: Go-to-market targeting, prioritization decks, ICP workshops.

Inputs to gather: Product description, known customer data (if any), business model.

Validation: Reject any segment defined only by demographics. Each must have a distinct job-to-be-done.

Prompt 
Segment the customer base for [PRODUCT] into 4–6 groups.

Each segment must include:
- Name (descriptive, not generic)
- Core job-to-be-done (what are they hiring this product to do?)
- Buying trigger (what event makes them start looking?)
- Willingness to pay (relative: low / mid / high, with reasoning)
- Acquisition channel most likely to reach them
- Why this segment is meaningfully different from the others

Then rank the segments by (expected LTV × reachability) and recommend
the top 2 to target first. Justify the ranking.

Reject purely demographic segments ("women 25–34"). Every segment must
map to a behavior or situation.

SWOT Strategy

When to use: Strategic diagnostic, competitive positioning, board prep.

Inputs to gather: Company materials, recent news, competitor summary.

Validation: If an entry could apply to any generic competitor, cut it.

Prompt 
Produce a SWOT analysis for [COMPANY] given the materials below.

Rules:
- Every entry must be specific enough that it could NOT apply to a
  generic competitor in the same industry.
- Every entry must cite the source or observation it is grounded in.
- Label each entry as EVIDENCED (based on the materials) or INFERRED
  (reasonable implication). Err toward cutting INFERRED entries.
- Provide exactly 3 entries per quadrant — force yourself to prioritize.

After the SWOT, write 2 paragraphs:
1. The single most important strategic implication.
2. The 3 follow-up questions you would ask the client next.

Financial Modeling Logic Modeling

When to use: Before opening Excel. AI structures the model; you build it.

Inputs to gather: Business model, revenue streams, known unit economics, time horizon.

Validation: Sanity-check driver ranges against comparable businesses before modeling.

Prompt 
Design the structure of a 3-statement financial model for [COMPANY].
I will build it in Excel — you are specifying the logic.

Provide:
1. Revenue model: every stream, its volume driver, and its price driver.
2. Cost model: fixed vs. variable costs, each tied to a driver.
3. Working capital assumptions: DSO, DPO, inventory days.
4. CapEx schedule and depreciation approach.
5. Cash flow bridge: how net income reconciles to cash.
6. Sensitivity inputs: the 3 drivers most worth flexing, with
   realistic low/base/high values and reasoning for each range.

Call out any assumption I will need external data to ground, and
suggest where to find it (10-K, industry benchmark, etc).

Interview Synthesis Qualitative

When to use: Primary research projects, customer discovery, expert interviews.

Inputs to gather: Transcripts (use an approved transcription tool), interviewee roles, research questions.

Validation: Spot-check 3 quotes against the source transcripts before using. AI occasionally paraphrases.

Prompt 
Given these [N] interview transcripts, extract:

1. Top 5 recurring themes, ranked by frequency of mention.
2. For each theme: one representative verbatim quote + speaker role.
3. Dissenting views: any themes where interviewees disagreed, with
   both sides quoted.
4. Weak signals: themes mentioned by only 1 interviewee but that seem
   potentially important — label explicitly as weak signal.
5. Open questions for follow-up interviews.

Rules:
- Do not invent or paraphrase quotes. If you cannot find a verbatim,
  say so.
- Do not merge themes to reach 5 — list fewer if the data supports
  fewer.
- Preserve speaker anonymity (use role labels, not names).
