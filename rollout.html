Chapter Rollout Guide

TAMID's existing structure already provides the distribution mechanism. The Director of Consulting passes the playbook down; Project Leads brief their members; project teams use it on real engagements. No special programming, no committees, no semester-long timeline.

The whole rollout in one sentence

Director of Consulting sends the playbook to Project Leads at semester kickoff. Project Leads read it and brief their teams. Project teams use it on client work.

Three roles, three responsibilities

Each role has one job. The whole system collapses if any of the three is skipped, and works automatically if all three are done.

Director of Consulting

One job: distribute the playbook to every Project Lead before projects begin.

  • Send the link at the semester kickoff meeting.
  • Set the expectation: "Read this before your first project meeting."
  • Surface the one or two parts of the playbook most relevant to this semester's project mix.

Project Lead

One job: internalize the playbook and brief the team on it.

  • Read the playbook end-to-end before the first project meeting.
  • Walk the team through the sections that apply to the engagement (e.g., competitive analysis if it's a market entry brief).
  • Pick which prompts from the library the team will lean on for this project.

Project Member

One job: apply it to real work.

  • Use the recommended models for the right tasks.
  • Pull from the prompt library instead of writing prompts from scratch.
  • Follow the ethical guardrails — source grading, no confidential data in public models.

Director's kickoff message — template

A starting point for the Director of Consulting's first message of the semester. Copy, customize, send.

Subject: AI Playbook for this semester's projects

Template 
Project Leads —

Before your first project meeting, please read through the TAMID
Consulting AI Playbook:

[LINK]

It covers model selection (which AI to use for what), prompt patterns
for the most common consulting tasks, and the ethical guardrails for
client work. Most of you are already using AI in some form — the goal
of the playbook is to make that usage consistent and defensible across
the chapter.

Two things to do before kickoff:
1. Read it end-to-end (about 20 minutes).
2. Pick the two or three sections most relevant to your project's
   scope, and walk your team through them at your first meeting.

If you have questions or want to flag something the playbook is
missing, reply here.

— [Director of Consulting]

What a successful rollout looks like

Three observable signals at the end of the semester.

Signal 1

Project Leads can name the right tool for any task

When a member asks "what should I use for [task]?", the lead has a confident, specific answer.

Signal 2

Numbers in deliverables are sourced

Source-grading shows up in the work. Unsourced claims become the exception, not the norm.

Signal 3

Teams use the prompt library

Prompts get pulled from the library instead of written from scratch. New prompts get suggested back.
